Call Volumes and Proportions for Mobile Crisis Response In Eugene and Springfield, Oregon

Background and Motivation

In recent years, Eugene has undergone two major changes in city-wide mental health and crisis care response. First, the rollout of MCSLC as an alternative crisis care service in 2024 and Second, the closure of CAHOOTS in Eugene in 2025. Together, these changes in service fundamentally altered the mechanisms used by citizens of Eugene to access emergency mental health care. Given the magnitude of Eugene’s shift in service and the significant differences between the CAHOOTS and MCSLC models in terms of operation and dispatch procedure, many questions have been raised regarding what effect these changes have had on the city


In this project, the following questions will be explored:


- How have call volumes and proportions historically been distributed between the EPD/SPD, CAHOOTS, and MCSLC?

- What effect did the closure of CAHOOTS in Eugene have on these volumes and proportions?

- How were the volumes and proportions of the most common call types handled by CAHOOTS impacted by the closure?

By analyzing call volumes and proportions it is possible to investigate how the public has responded to this change in service. Taking into account how rapid the transition from CAHOOTS to MCSLC has been, it is critical to understand how the public has reacted to the change and if an acceptable form of alternative care is still present in Eugene. Understanding this transition can help build an understanding of what is currently working well and where areas that need improvement might be.


Data

In order to study call volume and proportion in Eugene and Springfield, three main data sources were required. All data used in this project was drawn from public record and provided by Dr. Rori Rohlfs.


Eugene CAD Data:

This dataset contains information on 911 calls made in Eugene. For this project, information regarding primary units, call times, and call types for each record will be used. This information will make it possible to identify call volume trends for the Eugene Police Department as well as for CAHOOTS.

One area of concern in this dataset is inconsistency in naming conventions for prime units and call types from year to year. This increases the probability of accidentally omitting chunks of information at different stages of analysis.

SPD Calls for Service:

This dataset contains information on 911 calls and dispatches in the city of Springfield. Information on primary responding units, call times, and call types for each record will be used to analyze call volumes for the Springfield Police Department as well as for CAHOOTS.

Similarly to the Eugene CAD data, inconsistencies in primary unit and call type naming conventions could prove to be an issue.

MCSLC Calls:

This dataset will provide information on calls made to MCSLC from 2024 to 2025. information on call times and call types will be used to analyze call volumes in Eugene and Springfield.

One major hurdle to consider while working with this dataset is the fact that multiple call types are listed for each record. This greatly increases the likelihood of missing difficult to classify edge cases while looking into specific call types. Additionally, the fact that this dataset covers a significantly shorter time frame compared to the 911 data for Eugene and Springfield. This could potentially weaken the validity of any comparisons made across datasets.

Data Preperation and Cleaning

1. Read each dataset into a jupyter notebook as a data frame


2. Create a simplified date column for the three data frames that represent the year and month of every call recorded (ex. 2022-03). This will make aggregation easier during the following steps


3. Subset MCSLC data into Eugene and Springfield dataframes using the “City” column variables.


4. Use known CAHOOTS unit codes to subset the Eugene and Springfield emergency call data frames into EPD, SPD, and CAHOOTS data frames.


5. Group the CAHOOTS dataframes by call type and sort by count to identify the most commonly occurring call types.


6. Create total monthly call volume data frames for Eugene and Springfield


7. Use the values in the monthly call volume data frames to calculate the proportion of monthly calls handled by each agency in each city.

Results

Overall Pre-Analysis Trends

Prior to carrying out any formal analysis, there are a handful of important trends that can be observed in the data. Looking first at volume in Eugene, it can be seen that the EPD handles by far the most calls, typically floating around 10,000 calls per month. It can also be seen that CAHOOTS typically handled around 2000 monthly calls at their peak in Eugene. In 2024, the initial rollout of MCSLC took place. This figure shows call volumes around 400 calls per month. By comparison, Springfield takes fewer calls overall than Eugene, but shows similar patterns in terms of volume. In Springfield The SPD takes around 4000 calls per month, CAHOOTS takes around 400 calls per month, and MCSLC takes around 50 calls per month.

While call volume gives a good sense of the overall numbers, it is difficult to see the immediate effect of the CAHOOTS closure in Eugene. Shifting focus to monthly call proportions, another side of the story can be observed. When looking at call proportion, it becomes clear how similar the trends between Eugene and Springfield actually are. In both cities, the proportion of calls handled by the EPD/SPD stays mostly in the mid 80s to mid 90s, while the proportion of calls handled by CAHOOTS show a slow rise in proportion up to about 17% at their peak. In both cities, MCSLC appears to stay below 5% of monthly calls. By far the most important finding from this figure is the clear increase in EPD calls immediately following the closure of CAHOOTS in Eugene.

After running DiD analysis on the overall volumes and proportions of EPD and SPD calls, some of the observations above can be seen more clearly. In terms of volume, there doesn’t appear to be much of a change following the closure of CAHOOTS in Eugene. In fact, we can see that the effect of the closure was not statistically significant, with a p-value of 0.247. Call proportion on the other hand shows a significant leap of about 9% in Eugene immediately following the closure. Compared to the roughly 2% increase in Springfield, these results suggest a significant shift in service. Additionally, this effect turned out to be statistically significant, with a p-value below 0.000.

Switching focus to MCSLC, it is immediately clear that the volumes and proportions are much lower than those of the EPD and SPD. In Eugene, there is an increase of roughly 300 monthly calls post CAHOOTS closure and about a 2% increase in terms of proportion.

In Springfield, there is a very slight increase in volume of around 50 calls per month and only about a 0.5% increase in terms of proportion. This is likely due to the overall lower figures in Springfield as well as the fact that CAHOOTS remains operational in the city.

Discussion and Next Steps

With all this mind, there are some significant caveats to consider with this project. The biggest area of concern is the lack of data regarding MCSLC. Given that at the time of this project there is only about a year's worth of available data to work with, it’s difficult to say for sure how stable the trends observed really are. If this same analysis were to be carried out 5 years from now, the results might be more reliable.

Additionally, the differences in call type classification across agencies makes accurate comparisons difficult to achieve. While overall trends could potentially yield accurate insight, getting into specific call types introduces a lot more opportunity for errors and inaccuracy.

This project could likely be improved by shifting away from specific call types and looking at more generalizable variables such as call outcomes(ex. arrest, stayed in community). By focussing on variables with more clearly defined bounds, it would be easier to make assumptions and insights with a higher degree of confidence in the data.
